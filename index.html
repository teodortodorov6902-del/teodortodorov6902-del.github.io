Project Overview

The project aimed to build a model autonomous EV capable of driving forward, reversing, turning and stopping based on its surroundings. The platform demonstrates how sensing, control and actuation can come together to support future autonomous public transport.

Aims

We defined three main goals:

  • Robust line following using a dedicated lane.
  • Smooth, predictable manoeuvres using PWM-controlled DC motors.
  • Automatic braking when obstacles are detected ahead.

Context

Autonomous EVs can reduce congestion, improve safety and support climate targets by enabling cleaner, more efficient transport. Our scaled prototype mirrors the core subsystems of real self-driving shuttles and buses.

My Focus

I focused on motor characterisation, ultrasonic sensing, line-following behaviour analysis and documentation – using data-driven methods (ANOVA, Pearson correlation, curve fitting) to validate and tune system performance.

System Architecture

The EV is built around a microcontroller-driven architecture that coordinates sensing, decision-making and actuation. Below is a high-level view of the main layers.

Control Layer

A BBC Micro:bit runs MicroPython firmware to handle sensor polling, PWM generation and decision logic. The controller communicates with the motor drivers and reacts in real time to the EV’s environment.

MicroPython Event-driven logic

Sensing Layer

An ultrasonic sensor provides distance measurements, while a dual phototransistor array underneath the chassis detects the black line. RF measurements were also taken using the Micro:bit radio for time & frequency-domain analysis.

Actuation Layer

Four DC motors driven by a dual H-bridge (DRV8833) provide propulsion and steering through differential speeds. A continuous-rotation servo sweeps the ultrasonic sensor to widen the field of view.

Mechanical & Mounting

A custom 3D-printed SolidWorks bracket secures the ultrasonic sensor to the servo, designed with ±0.5 mm tolerances to account for fabrication and component tolerances.

Subsystems in Detail

Each subsystem was tested individually and then integrated into the final vehicle. Below are highlights from the most important components.

DC Motors & PWM Control

We characterised four identical DC motors across duty cycles from 20–100% using a bench supply, multimeter and oscilloscope. A one-way ANOVA showed significant differences between motor positions, revealing that the rear motors drew slightly more power due to additional load from the controller and robotics board.

  • Duty-cycle range: 20–100%.
  • Power variability highest at 20% (CV = 12.3%).
  • Near-perfect alignment at full load (CV = 0.2%).

Ultrasonic Distance Sensing

The ultrasonic sensor was tested at known distances and the echo pulse width was monitored on an oscilloscope. Using the speed of sound, measured pulse widths were converted into calculated distances and compared against ground truth.

  • Correlation between measured and calculated distance: r = 0.99972.
  • Reliable detection over the operating range used by the EV.
  • Additional tests with different materials to assess surface dependency.

Line-Following Optical System

A dual phototransistor board underneath the chassis distinguishes between black tape and lighter surfaces. Analogue readings are converted into a left–right intensity difference which directly maps to steering commands.

A cubic polynomial fit was used to model the relationship between lateral displacement and sensor difference, and the derivative of this function provided a measure of turning sensitivity at various positions.

Servo Platform & Mechanical Design

The ultrasonic sensor is mounted on a continuous-rotation servo to scan the environment. I used a micrometre to measure transducer spacing, PCB length and servo horn hole diameters, then designed a bracket with carefully chosen tolerances for a secure but non-binding fit.

  • Material: PLA via BCN3D Epsilon W27.
  • Orientation optimised for strength along the print direction.
  • Post-processing with a curved file to fine-tune screw-hole diameters.

Servo Characterisation

The servo’s current draw was recorded at 60°, 90°, 120°, 150° and 180°. Results showed a strong positive correlation between angle and current with an almost linear power increase under constant 4.8 V supply.

  • Correlation (angle vs current): r ≈ 0.993.
  • Power from 0.60 W (60°) to 0.77 W (180°).
  • Non-linear rise at large angles hints at higher torque demands.

RF Communication Analysis

The Micro:bit’s 2.4 GHz radio was examined both in the time domain using a diode detector and oscilloscope, and in the frequency domain using a spectrum analyser. This validated transmission timing, bandwidth and centre frequency.

  • Centre frequency around 2.407 GHz.
  • Approx. 1 MHz bandwidth, narrowband behaviour.
  • Signal strength from –40 dBm to –70 dBm depending on setup.

Data-Driven Results

Quantitative analysis played a key role in validating each subsystem and tuning the EV’s overall behaviour.

Statistical Analysis

A one-way ANOVA was used to study power consumption differences between motor positions. The F-test indicated that at least one motor behaved differently, guiding our understanding of load distribution and informing control tuning.

Stopping Distance & Reaction Time

Reaction time and stopping distance were measured for varying duty cycles and threshold distances. As duty cycle increased, reaction time decreased, confirming the system’s ability to adapt to speed while maintaining collision avoidance.

Line-Following Behaviour

The cubic model of sensor difference versus lateral position allowed us to reason about stability. The derivative revealed weak response near the centre line but stronger corrective action at larger deviations, reflecting a “soft” control law around the desired path.

Project Timeline

The work was spread across the academic year, combining weekly lab sessions with independent development and analysis.

Weeks 1–6

Initial familiarisation with Micro:bit and robotics board, basic DC motor tests, early experiments with sensors and environment setup.

Weeks 7–12

Integration of ultrasonic sensor and DC motors, initial line-following behaviour, and submission of a progress video demonstrating partial functionality.

Weeks 13–20
Full Integration & Performance Tuning

Fine-tuning PWM parameters, improving turn stability, validating stopping distance at multiple speeds, and iterating on mechanical design.

Weeks 21–End
Data Analysis & Final Report

Performing formal statistical analysis, generating plots, interpreting results and compiling the final technical report and presentation.
